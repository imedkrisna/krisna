Part 1 – The instruments and the logic

Import quota – mechanics

  • Government caps the quantity that can be imported.

  • Effects in a small open economy with a binding quota:

    • Domestic price rises above the world price.
    • Same consumer loss and producer gain triangles as a tariff.
    • Difference: the rectangle that would be tariff revenue becomes a quota rent – captured by whoever holds the import licence.

  • Three rent destinations:

    1. Auctioned licences \(\rightarrow\) government (= tariff).
    2. Allocated licences \(\rightarrow\) politically chosen importers.
    3. Foreign exporters (next slide) \(\rightarrow\) worst case.

Quota vs tariff: when do they diverge?

Tariff and quota look equivalent in a static, perfectly-competitive textbook. They diverge when:

  • Demand grows. Quota stays fixed \(\rightarrow\) implicit protection rises. Tariff rate stays fixed \(\rightarrow\) protection erodes.

  • Domestic firm has market power. Quota gives the domestic monopolist a guaranteed protected market; tariff still leaves contestability from imports.

  • Rent allocation matters politically. Quota allocations are administratively granted – a magnet for rent-seeking.

  • Voluntary Export Restraint (VER): quota administered by the exporter. Japan-US autos, 1981. The quota rent flows abroad. Strictly worse than a tariff for the home country – yet politically convenient. Banned by the WTO Safeguards Agreement Art. 11 (1995).

Non-Tariff Measures (NTMs): the universe

  • UNCTAD MAST classification (2019) – 16 chapters, hundreds of categories.

    • Technical: SPS (sanitary), TBT (technical), pre-shipment inspection.
    • Non-technical: price controls, contingent measures (AD, CVD, safeguards), licences/quotas/bans, finance, distribution restrictions, government procurement, IP, rules of origin, subsidies, export measures.

  • Stylised fact: as average tariffs fell from ~30% (1980s) to ~10% today in developing countries, the count of NTMs has risen.

  • Hard to compare across countries because:

    • Many are not notified.
    • Tariff equivalents vary by product, firm, route.

Local Content Requirement (LCR / TKDN)

  • Mandate: a minimum share of the value (or of specified components) must be sourced domestically.

  • Indonesia uses LCRs heavily:

    • TKDN handsets – 4G/5G smartphones must meet ~35% TKDN to be sold; pushed Apple to negotiate, eventually invest.
    • EV batteries / EVs – staircase to 80% TKDN.
    • Solar PV, oil & gas services, government procurement.

  • Static cost: forces use of more expensive / lower-quality domestic inputs \(\rightarrow\) tariff-equivalent often double-digit.

  • Claimed dynamic benefit: linkages, learning, technology transfer.

  • WTO status: TRIMs Agreement (1995) prohibits LCRs in goods. Honoured selectively; many WTO members maintain creative variants.

Standards: SPS and TBT

  • Legitimate roles: information asymmetry (consumer cannot observe quality), externalities (pesticide residue, emissions), network compatibility.

  • Protectionist temptation: a strict standard no foreign supplier can meet today keeps competitors out, perfectly legally.

  • Evidence for Indonesia (Elisabeth & Verico, 2022, JITLP): TBT measures have a non-trivial dampening effect on Indonesia’s manufacturing exports – the same instruments work both ways.

  • Trade-off:

    • Tighter standard \(\rightarrow\) fewer entrants \(\rightarrow\) higher minimum quality, less price competition.
    • Optimal tightness depends on the externality, not on what protects domestic firms.

Subsidies and the industrial-policy revival

  • In GTA, subsidies (financial grant + state loan + state aid + trade finance) are the single largest distortive category by count.

  • Post-2020 surge driven by:

    • United States – IRA (USD 369bn, 2022), CHIPS Act (USD 52bn, 2022).
    • EU – Green Deal Industrial Plan, Net-Zero Industry Act, CBAM.
    • China – continued support to chips, EVs, batteries; “new productive forces”.
    • Indonesia – tax holidays for smelters, KEK incentives, biodiesel mandate financed by the CPO Fund.

  • WTO disciplines (SCM Agreement) cover subsidies for goods, but actionability is high-bar and the dispute pipeline is jammed.

Contingent protection

The “WTO-legal” way to raise protection after the schedules are bound:

  • Anti-dumping (AD) – duty if foreign firm sells “below normal value” and causes injury. ~4,000 distortive measures in GTA. Procedure-heavy, easy to abuse, the most-used trade defence worldwide.

  • Anti-subsidy (CVD) – countervailing duty against subsidised imports. ~850 measures.

  • Safeguards – emergency duty/quota against an import surge causing serious injury. Non-discriminatory in principle, time-limited, requires compensation to affected exporters.

Indonesia uses all three; Indonesia is also a target of dozens of foreign AD cases (palm-oil derivatives, paper, steel).

Export-side measures

Export taxes, bans, licensing, price benchmarks. Why does an exporter restrict its own exports?

  1. Revenue. Easy tax base on commodities (Indonesia palm-oil export levy \(\rightarrow\) BPDPKS / CPO Fund).

  2. Domestic price stabilisation. Crisis bans on rice, cooking-oil exports (2022).

  3. Downstream development. Indonesia nickel-ore export ban (2014, then 2020) \(\rightarrow\) smelter and battery investment.

  4. Strategic / geopolitical. Critical-minerals controls (rare earths, gallium, germanium).

  • Welfare logic: a large supplier can shift world prices to its favour – a terms-of-trade gain. A small supplier just loses revenue.

  • Downstream story works only if the downstream industry is genuinely viable – otherwise it is a transfer to a subsidised loss-maker.

Part 2 – Why use NTMs at all?

The political-economy answer

  • Tariffs sit in a public schedule – consumers, downstream users and trade partners can read them.

  • NTMs are opaque:

    • Tariff equivalents must be estimated, often product-by-product.
    • Compliance cost is borne by the firm, not measured in the budget.

  • Concentrated benefits, dispersed costs (Olson, 1965): the gainers organise, the losers do not.

  • Indonesia food-NTMs estimated ~49% tariff-equivalent (The Conversation, 2022) – a number no parliament would approve as an explicit tariff.

The economic-rationale answer

Some NTMs address real market failures:

  • Information – product standards, labelling.

  • Externalities – emissions, pesticide residue, financial-stability spillovers. Carbon Border Adjustment (EU CBAM) is the canonical recent case.

  • Strategic competitiveness / appropriability – subsidies for R&D-intensive industries where private investment under-delivers.

  • Resilience – post-COVID supply-chain redundancy; pandemic stockpiles; semiconductors.

The honest version of the infant-industry argument lives here too. The catch from last week still applies: a domestic policy is almost always a better instrument than a trade policy for a domestic problem.

The geopolitical answer (post-2018)

  • US export controls on advanced semiconductors to China (2022 Oct, 2023 Oct).

  • Inward FDI screening: CFIUS (US), EU FDI Regulation, UK NSI Act, Indonesia DNI revisions.

  • “Friend-shoring”, “de-risking”, critical-minerals partnerships.

  • US 2025 tariff package (broad, escalatory, partly outside WTO process).

  • The policy paradigm has tilted from “open by default, intervene if needed” to “managed by default, liberalise if convenient”. The 2022-25 distortive count in GTA confirms it.

Part 3 – The World Trade Organization

Why multilateral rules?

The cautionary tale of the 1930s:

  • Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act (US, 1930) – average tariff on dutiable imports raised to ~59%.

  • Retaliation – Canada, France, UK, others raised their tariffs in turn.

  • World trade collapsed by roughly two-thirds, 1929-1933.

  • Beggar-thy-neighbour devaluations and import controls deepened the Depression.

  • Lesson taken at Bretton Woods (1944): tie hands collectively so no single government’s domestic politics can unleash a global trade war.

GATT 1947: a “provisional” treaty that lasted 47 years

  • Three Bretton Woods siblings designed: IMF (money), World Bank (reconstruction), ITO (trade).

  • ITO Charter (Havana, 1948) was rejected by the US Senate in 1950 \(\rightarrow\) ITO never born.

  • Its tariff-negotiation chapter survived as the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT) – signed by 23 contracting parties, applied “provisionally” from 1 January 1948.

  • “Provisionally” until 1995.

GATT core principles

Four principles still anchor today’s WTO:

  1. Most-Favoured Nation (Art. I). Any concession granted to one member is granted to all members. (Exceptions: FTAs, GSP, balance-of-payments.)

  2. National Treatment (Art. III). Once an import has cleared customs, it must be treated no worse than a like domestic product (taxes, regulations).

  3. Tariff bindings. Each member has a schedule of bound (ceiling) rates; applied rates can be lower but not higher.

  4. Reciprocity. Concessions are exchanged, not given away – liberalisation is a negotiated political deal.

From GATT rounds to the WTO

  • 8 GATT rounds, 1947-1994. Early rounds: tariff cuts on industrial goods.

  • Tokyo Round (1973-79): first serious push on non-tariff measures – voluntary “codes” on subsidies, AD, standards, government procurement, customs valuation.

  • Uruguay Round (1986-94): the big bang.

    • Agriculture brought in (AoA).
    • Textiles brought in (ATC, phase-out of MFA).
    • Services (GATS), intellectual property (TRIPS), TRIMs.
    • Strengthened, binding dispute settlement (DSU).
    • WTO created, in force 1 January 1995.

What the WTO actually is

  • Member-driven. 164 members, ~98% of world trade. Indonesia is a founding member.

  • Three legs:

    • GATT (goods), GATS (services), TRIPS (intellectual property).

  • Plus a Dispute Settlement Body – panels + Appellate Body, with binding rulings and authorised retaliation. For 25 years, the “crown jewel”.

  • No real legislative capacity: change the rules only by member consensus.

Doha “Development” Round: the agenda

  • Launched November 2001, Doha Ministerial – two months after 9/11, framed as a deliberate signal that the world could still cooperate.

  • Called the “Development Round” in response to developing-country complaints about the Uruguay outcomes:

    • TRIPS pushed up the cost of medicines and technology.
    • Agriculture liberalisation in rich countries had barely begun.
    • “Implementation issues” – promised technical assistance never materialised.

  • Built on the single undertaking – “nothing is agreed until everything is agreed” – forcing cross-issue trade-offs.

  • Eight negotiating tracks: agriculture, NAMA (industrial tariffs), services (GATS), TRIPS & public health, Singapore issues (investment, competition, procurement, trade facilitation), rules (AD/SCM, FTAs), environment, special & differential treatment.

Doha: the slow collapse, 2003-2017

  • Cancún 2003 (MC5): collapsed. Singapore issues + the cotton initiative (4 West African states demanding the end of US cotton subsidies). Birth of the G20 trade bloc – Brazil, India, South Africa, China – as a counterweight to the US-EU duopoly.

  • Hong Kong 2005 (MC6): agreed to phase out agricultural export subsidies.

  • Geneva July 2008 mini-ministerial: very close to a deal. Broke down over the Special Safeguard Mechanism (SSM): India insisted on a low trigger to protect subsistence farmers from import surges; US refused. One issue, one deal-breaker.

  • Bali 2013 (MC9): salvaged the Trade Facilitation Agreement – first multilateral deal born under the WTO.

  • Nairobi 2015 (MC10): end of agricultural export subsidies formally agreed, but the US and EU declined to recommit to the Doha mandate. Post-Doha era begins.

  • Three deeper reasons for failure: (i) single undertaking made a deal infeasible; (ii) China’s 2001 accession transformed the negotiating arithmetic; (iii) the US pivoted to mega-regionals.

Plan B: the rise of FTAs

  • Members responded by going bilateral / regional:

    • CPTPP (2018), USMCA (2020), RCEP (2022), AfCFTA (2021), IPEF (2022).
    • EU FTA pipeline (Mercosur, Indonesia IEU-CEPA, Vietnam, Korea, Japan).

  • Plurilateral Joint Statement Initiatives inside the WTO: e-commerce, services domestic regulation, investment facilitation.

  • For Indonesia: founder of ASEAN Economic Community; member of RCEP; IEU-CEPA after 19 negotiation rounds (2016-2025).

  • Cost of multilateral failure: a “spaghetti bowl” of overlapping FTAs with different rules of origin – compliance burden falls on firms.

The WTO’s current crisis

  • Appellate Body paralysed since 11 December 2019. US (under both parties) has blocked new appointments since 2017, citing judicial overreach.

  • Without a functioning AB, a losing party can appeal “into the void” and the ruling never becomes binding.

  • Workaround: 26 members (incl. EU, China, Australia) joined the Multi-Party Interim Appeal Arrangement (MPIA). Indonesia and the US have not joined.

  • US 2025 tariff package proceeded largely outside WTO-consistent process.

  • The discipline that took 50 years to build is being eroded faster than it can be defended.

Indonesia at the WTO

  • DS592 – Indonesia (Nickel ore export ban), 2019 -.

    • EU complaint against the 2020 ore-export ban and related domestic-processing requirements.
    • Panel report (Nov 2022): Indonesia’s measures inconsistent with GATT Art. XI:1 (quantitative restrictions); justifications under Art. XX (general exceptions) rejected.
    • Indonesia appealed – into the void. No binding outcome. The ban remains in force.

  • Other live or recent disputes: chicken (DS484, won by Brazil), bioethanol AD, several palm-oil disputes against the EU.

  • Indonesia is both a beneficiary of the rules and a frequent test case for their limits.

Part 4 – What governments actually use

Global Trade Alert (GTA / NIPO)

  • Independent monitoring database of unilateral trade and industrial policy interventions, run from St. Gallen.

  • Scope:

    • Coverage starts 1 November 2008.
    • Discriminatory measures only – a uniform VAT change is excluded.
    • Unilateral – FTAs, RCEP, EU accession schedules are not in here.
    • Not WTO-notified TBT/SPS, not UN sanctions, not CITES.
    • Subsidies below USD 10M (USD 100M for SME-only) excluded.

  • Each record is coded by instrument, sector, affected partner, and an “initial assessment” (distortive vs liberalising).

  • Used today: April 2026 inventory, 53,102 measures.

Most-used instruments, 2009–2025

Tariffs lead, but subsidies (financial grant + state loan + state aid + trade finance) dominate when summed. Anti-dumping is the largest single trade-defence tool.

Distortive vs liberalising over time

Inflection points: 2009 (post-GFC), 2018 (US-China), 2020 (COVID), 2022+ (industrial-policy revival – IRA, CHIPS, EU CBAM).

Who intervenes most?

The big interveners are big and rich, not poor. Industrial policy is a rich-country game; developing countries follow.

Indonesia: top instruments

Note the export side – export tax, export price benchmark, export ban, export licensing. Distinctive for a resource-exporting economy.

Indonesia: pace of intervention

Of 502 Indonesian measures since 2009: ~66% distortive, ~34% liberalising. Notable: 2009 commodity-boom export controls; 2014 mineral-ore export ban; 2020 nickel ore ban; 2024-25 hilirisasi continues.

Wrap-up

Take-aways

  1. Tariffs are visible. The real action is everywhere else – subsidies, LCRs, standards, contingent protection, export controls.

  2. NTMs have legitimate uses (information, externalities, resilience) and high abuse potential (opacity, rent-seeking).

  3. The WTO was the post-war answer to 1930s-style trade collapse. Its negotiation function stalled in 2008; its adjudication function broke in 2019; its rule-book still binds, weakly.

  4. For Indonesia the question is not whether to intervene – we already do, ~30 measures a year on average – but whether to intervene well or badly. That is what this course is for.
